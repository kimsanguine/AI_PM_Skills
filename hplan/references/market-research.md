# Market Research Gate

Use this reference for Gate 1 market diagnosis and competitor research.

## Required Outputs

- Market diagnosis in 5 sentences
- Competitor / alternative research summary
- "What we will not build" table
- Market vacuum / wedge paragraph
- 4-axis positioning map when useful
- Counter position, 3 options
- Final hero copy
- Validation questions for interviews
- Human Evidence Checkpoint

## Market Diagnosis Pattern

Write the diagnosis around the user's paid outcome, not the feature category.

Use this structure:

1. What is already abundant in the market?
2. Where does the user still get stuck despite those tools?
3. Which current alternatives or workarounds prove the pain exists?
4. What productive outcome would someone pay for?
5. What narrow market vacuum should this product enter first?

## Market Vacuum Stress Test

Do not define the market only by category name. Look for a vacuum where multiple dimensions overlap.

Useful axes:

- Existing education/content vs actual product usage
- Tool usage vs judgment/problem-definition ability
- Building/prototyping vs monetization
- Global generic market vs local/niche market
- Product feature vs paid outcome

Use this table when positioning is unclear:

| Axis | Existing market is abundant in | Still missing | Evidence needed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Thinking / judgment |  |  |  |
| Building / execution |  |  |  |
| Monetization / business model |  |  |  |
| Local / niche context |  |  |  |

The product should enter the smallest credible vacuum, not the largest attractive category.

## Competitor Research Rules

Competitor research is mandatory before build decisions.

Include:

- Direct competitors
- Indirect alternatives
- Manual workarounds
- Agencies, templates, spreadsheets, internal tools, ChatGPT chats, Notion docs, or existing services
- Pricing and packaging when relevant
- User complaints, review patterns, or unmet workflow gaps

When the user asks for actual current competitors or pricing, browse the web and cite sources. Prefer official websites, documentation, pricing pages, review pages, app marketplaces, and recent public posts.

## Do Not

- Do not define the market as a broad category like "AI productivity" or "creator tools" without a narrow paid outcome.
- Do not claim a market vacuum just because the user has not personally seen a competitor.
- Do not treat competitor weakness as proof of demand; it only creates interview hypotheses.
- Do not use "all-in-one," "better AI," "easier," or "faster" as the wedge unless user evidence names the exact failure mode.
- Do not copy a competitor's feature set into the MVP. Start from the painful workaround and switching condition.
- Do not position against tool usage alone when the real gap is judgment, workflow ownership, cost control, or trust.
- Do not hard-code time-sensitive market claims. Browse and cite when current market facts, pricing, or competitor status matter.

## Wedge Table

Use this table:

| Competitor / Alternative | What it solves | Where users still struggle | Our wedge | Evidence needed |
|---|---|---|---|---|

The wedge must be narrower than "better AI" or "all-in-one." Good wedges are local language quality, workflow-specific output, regulated domain, cost control, reviewability, distribution channel, or faster time-to-value.

## What Not To Build

Use this table:

| Do not build | Why | Instead build / validate |
|---|---|---|

Use it to cut scope. If the product has high variable AI cost, explicitly reject unlimited usage until COGS is measured.

State what the product deliberately does not teach, automate, or own. Clear exclusions sharpen the wedge and prevent generic "all-in-one" positioning.

## Counter Position

Generate three counter-position options:

- Local or niche wedge
- Outcome/workflow wedge
- Economic or operational wedge

Then choose one final position and explain the tradeoff.

The counter position should say what common market belief the product rejects. Good patterns:

- "The problem is not lack of tools; it is lack of judgment."
- "The problem is not building many things; it is finding one painful paid outcome."
- "The problem is not AI output; it is reviewable decisions with evidence."

Do not copy these literally unless they fit the user's market.

## Hero Copy

Hero copy must name the outcome.

Good:

> Turn scattered intake inputs into a review-ready decision brief with evidence, risk notes, and approval controls.

Weak:

> AI-powered content creation platform.

## Validation Questions

End Gate 1 market diagnosis with 5 validation questions that flow into user interviews:

- Who had this problem recently?
- What current workaround did they use?
- What cost, time loss, risk, or missed revenue appeared?
- What would make them switch?
- What price or commitment signal is realistic?

## Human Evidence Checkpoint

End with a decision block:

- Does the human accept this competitor set?
- Does the human accept the proposed market vacuum?
- Which counter position should be tested first?
- Which ICP should be recruited for 5 interviews?
- What would change the decision from `interview` to `build`?
